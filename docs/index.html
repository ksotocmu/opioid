The Opioid Crisis

In the 21st century, nearly 450,000 Americans have died from an opioid overdose.[1] This exceeds the number of American military deaths in World War II. Every day, around 10 Pennsylvanians die from a drug overdose,[2] the majority involving prescription (e.g. OxyContin) or illicit (e.g. Fentanyl) opioids.

This crisis began with the routine prescription of easily abusable opioids such as Oxycodone in the late 1990s. The situation escalated around ten years ago with increased use of heroin as well as synthetic opioids like Fentanyl. Consequently, the rate of opioid overdose fatalities (deaths per 100,000 residents) has exploded in recent years. For every 100,000 Pennsylvanians, 23.8 died due to an opioid overdose in 2018.[3]

Despites its massive and ever-increasing death toll, this crisis received remarkably little attention in the national discourse for many years. While the opioid epidemic has finally become more salient in media, the resources devoted to this issue are dwarfed by the immensity of its carnage. Nationally, the number of opioid deaths has exceeded the number of traffic fatalities in some years. In 2018 alone, the epidemic cost an estimated $179 billion in negative externalities.[4]

Models

This project provides several models reflecting different data sets and different levels of model complexity.

County-Level Regression

Prediction: Annual Deaths per 100,000 due to Opioids

The opioid death rate in each of Pennsylvania's 67 counties is influenced by a number of factors. This model analyzes demographic data from the U.S. Census Bureau for each county to learn to predict its annual opioid death rate. Specifically, it considers income, employment, and poverty data for each county.

The model is a simple neural network for regression trained on county-level data from the Census Bureau and the CDC from the past 10 years. More detailed information can be found in Data + Methodology.

Check out model predictions for 2020.

Time Series Forecasting

Prediction: Daily Naloxone Administrations

Every day, first responders administer Naloxone (Narcan®) to people undergoing an opioid overdose. This model takes in data from the tens of thousands of Naloxone administrations performed in PA over the last three years. Using a sophisticated deep learning model, it identifies trends and recurrences to predict the number of future overdose incidents.
